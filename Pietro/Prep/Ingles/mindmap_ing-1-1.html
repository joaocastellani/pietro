The Power of Art Modal Verbs · First Conditional · Survey Report
1
Modal Verb — Should
  • Use: advice / recommendation (conselho / recomendação)
  • Never changes form — he/she/it should (nunca shoulds)
✅ Affirmative: subject + should + base verb "You should wear a coat." → Você deveria usar um casaco. ❌ Negative: subject + shouldn't + base verb "You shouldn't call them now." → Você não deveria ligar para eles agora. ❓ Interrogative: Should + subject + base verb? "Should they go on foot?" → Eles deveriam ir a pé?
⚠️ Trap: shouldn't ≠ mustn't. Shouldn't = not recommended. Mustn't = prohibited.

2
Modal Verb — Have to
  • Use: necessity / obligation (necessidade / obrigação)
  • 3rd person: she/he/it has to (conjuga!)
  • Don't have to = not necessary ≠ prohibited
✅ Affirmative: subject + have to/has to + base verb "You have to drive on the left in the UK." → Você é obrigado a dirigir pela esquerda no RU. ❌ Negative: don't/doesn't have to + base verb "Students don't have to finish today." → Os alunos não precisam terminar hoje. ❓ Interrogative: Do/Does + subject + have to? "Does she have to wear a uniform?" → Ela é obrigada a usar uniforme?
⚠️ Trap: don't have to ≠ prohibition. "You don't have to come" = not necessary, not forbidden.

3
First Conditional
  • Use: prediction or promise — real future condition
  • If-clause: Simple Present (NEVER will)
  • Result clause: will + base form
Structure: If + Simple Present + , + will + base verb "If museums charge expensive fees, access to art will be more difficult." → Se os museus cobrarem taxas caras, o acesso à arte será mais difícil. "If you don't water the plant, it will die." → Se você não regar a planta, ela vai morrer.
⚠️ Trap: NEVER "If it will rain…" ❌ → Always "If it rains…" ✅ The if-clause uses Simple Present only.

4
Should vs. Have to — Key Differences
  • Should = advice — person may or may not follow
  • Have to = external obligation — must comply
  • Shouldn't = not recommended (não recomendado)
  • Don't have to = not necessary (não é necessário)
  • Mustn't = prohibited (proibido) — different from both!
"You should eat vegetables." → advice → Você deveria comer legumes. (conselho) "You have to wear a seatbelt." → obligation → Você é obrigado a usar cinto. (obrigação legal) "You don't have to wear a tie." → not necessary → Não é necessário usar gravata.

💡 Golden Tips
Tip 1
Don't have to ≠ Mustn't
Don't have to = não é necessário (sem obrigação).
Mustn't = não pode (proibido).

"You don't have to wear a uniform." (opcional)
"You mustn't enter without a badge." (proibido)
Tip 2
Never "will" inside the if-clause
A cláusula com if usa sempre o Simple Present.

❌ "If it will rain…"
✅ "If it rains…"

Dica: if = condição presente → will = resultado futuro.
Tip 3
Should never changes form
Ao contrário de have to (→ has to na 3ª pessoa),
should nunca muda:

✅ "She should go." ✅ "They should go."
❌ "She shoulds go."
Tip 4
Comma in First Conditional
If-clause no início → use vírgula:
"If it rains, we will stay."

If-clause no final → sem vírgula:
"We will stay if it rains."
Tip 5
Survey Report: stay impersonal
Nunca use I think ou I believe em survey reports.

❌ "I think the festival was great."
✅ "The data shows that the festival attracted 115,000 people."

Use: according to, the report says, the data shows.
Tip 6
Pictogram = minimalist, not detailed
A questão QI-10 pergunta pela alternativa FALSA.

Pictogramas são simples e minimalistas — transcendem barreiras linguísticas.

❌ "Pictogram designers use complex elements full of details." → FALSA ✅
